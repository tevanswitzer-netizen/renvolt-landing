SECTION 1: EV CHARGING + BATTERY CO-LOCATION STRATEGY

Why This is the Right Business Idea

Market Reality Check

Alberta is experiencing an EV charging infrastructure explosion:

- Government funding: $1.7M in provincial grants (2025) + federal ZEVIP program allocating $5B+ nationally

- Over 325 new EV charging stations announced for Alberta in 2025-2026

- Undersaturation: Only ~1,000 EVs in Alberta as of 2025, but growth is projected at 15-20% annually

- Market timing: Early players can secure premium real estate before competitors move in

The Hidden Economics: Demand Charges

The real money is not in charging fees—it's in energy cost optimization. Here's why this matters:

Problem for charging operators:

- A 12-charger site drawing 150-350 kW simultaneously creates massive demand charges

- Utilities charge $8-20/kW/month for peak demand

- Annual demand charges: $12,000-$40,000+ per site

- Total operating cost: $120K-$150K/year on a $200K+ infrastructure investment

Our solution:

- Co-locate 100 kWh battery storage at every charging site

- Charge battery during off-peak hours (cheap electricity)

- Discharge during peak times (avoid expensive demand charges)

- Result: 50% reduction in demand charges = $6,000-$20,000/year savings per site

Multiple Revenue Streams (Not Just Charging)

This is critical to investor confidence. We don't rely on a single revenue source:

EV Charging Fees: $24,000/month (60 MWh @ $0.40/kWh) = $288,000/year

Demand Charge Savings (50% share): $3,000-$5,000/month = $36,000-$60,000/year

Grid Services Revenue: $2,000-$3,000/month = $24,000-$36,000/year

Energy Management SaaS: $500-$1,000/month = $6,000-$12,000/year

Total Blended Revenue per 12-Charger Site: $29,500-$33,000/month = $354,000-$396,000/year

Payback period: 9-14 months on $350K capex (chargers + battery + software)

Year 2+ annual margin: 40-50% (operational leverage kicks in)

Government Grants Cover 50%+ of Capex

This is a MAJOR investor advantage:

- Charger equipment: 50% subsidy via ZEVIP/provincial programs = effectively $100K cost on $200K hardware

- Battery installation: Emerging incentives for energy storage (federal Clean Tech ITC = 30%)

- Net capex required from Renvolt: $200K-$250K per site (vs. listed $350K)

- Government effectively subsidizing our capex before we deploy

This means:

- Payback improves to 7-10 months

- Less equity needed from investors

- De-risks project economics

Competitive Advantage & Scalability

1. Integrated hardware + software: Competitors (ChargePoint, Tesla Supercharger) focus on chargers, not optimization

2. Energy intelligence: Our software predicts peak times, optimizes battery discharge, maximizes revenue

3. Multi-revenue model: We capture value across charging, demand reduction, grid services, and software

Why This is a Sound Business Idea

Addressable Market: Industrial Peak Shaving

Alberta has approximately 500+ industrial/commercial facilities with:

- High-load operations (food processing, data centres, warehouses)

- Demand charges exceeding $10,000/month

- ESG mandates from parent companies or investors

- Need for grid resilience during peak hours

These customers want:

- Resilience (backup during outages)

- Cost reduction (demand charge management)

- ESG credentials (renewable/efficient power)

- ZERO capex (they don't want to own batteries)

The BaaS Model Solves for All Four Needs

Traditional approach (customer buys battery):

- Upfront cost: $500K+ for 100 kWh system

- Customer carries depreciation risk

- Customer maintains system

- Battery degrades; customer replaces in 7-10 years

Our BaaS approach:

- Zero customer capex (we own and install)

- Customer pays $3,500-$5,000/month subscription

- We handle all maintenance, monitoring, replacement

- We guarantee 99% uptime (SLA backing)

- Shared savings: Customer gets 50%, we get 50% of demand reduction benefits

Real BaaS Economics: 100 kWh System, 3-Year Agreement

Customer Perspective:

- Potential savings with battery: $18,000/year

- Our subscription: $42,000/year

- Net cost to customer: $24,000/year INITIALLY

Why they sign anyway:

- Resilience value: Battery provides backup during blackouts (critical for operations)

- ESG reporting: Company achieves carbon reduction target

- Scale economics: As battery ages and demand charges rise, breaks even after Year 2

- Lease accounting: OPEX is tax-advantaged vs. capex

- Current market: Batteries go to recyclers (crushed, low margin)

- Every solar installer wants cheaper batteries for add-ons
