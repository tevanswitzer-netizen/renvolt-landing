SECTION 2: PILLAR 1 - EV CHARGING + BATTERY CO-LOCATION STRATEGY

Market Opportunity:

Alberta's EV market is at an inflection point. Government funding is flowing, but infrastructure deployment lags demand. Charging operators face a hidden economics problem: they don't understand how to optimize energy costs.

Market Reality Check:

- Government funding: $1.7M in provincial grants (2025) + federal ZEVIP program allocating $5B+ nationally

- New stations announced: 325 EV charging stations planned for Alberta in 2025-2026

- Current undersaturation: Only ~1,000 EVs in Alberta as of 2025, but 15-20% annual growth projected

- Market timing: Early players can secure premium real estate before competitors arrive

The Hidden Economics: Demand Charges

The real money in EV charging isn't in charging fees—it's in energy cost optimization. Here's why this matters:

Problem for charging operators:

- A typical DC fast charger draws 200kW when operational

- Utility companies charge "demand charges" ($15-30/kW/month) based on the single highest 15-minute peak

- If a charger hits peak usage during grid peak hours, demand charges can be $3,000-6,000/month

- On a 10-station site, demand charges become $30,000-60,000/month = $360,000-720,000/year

- For a typical charging site with $500K/year revenue, demand charges consume 30-50% of gross profit

Our Solution: Battery Co-location + Smart Scheduling

- Deploy a 100-200kWh battery at each charging site

- During low-rate hours (2am-6am), pre-charge the battery from the grid

- During peak hours, discharge battery to power chargers, avoiding demand charge peaks

- Result: Reduce demand charges by 60-80%, converting costs into profit

Revenue Streams:

1. Charging Fees: $0.30-0.50/kWh (direct to Renvolt)

2. Demand Charge Optimization: $2,000-4,000/month per site (margin improvement)

3. Grid Services Revenue: $500-1,000/month per battery (selling frequency regulation to the grid)

4. Government Grants: Subsidize 40-50% of upfront capital costs

Pillar 1 Unit Economics:

- Capital cost per site: $200K (chargers + battery + controls)

- Government grant (net cost): $100K

- Monthly revenue: $3,000-5,000

- Monthly costs: $800-1,200

- Net monthly profit: $2,000-3,800

- Payback period: 30-50 months (investor-friendly)

- IRR: 25-35% annually

Go-to-Market Strategy:

- Target 1: Gas station/convenience store chains (want to add EV charging without capex)

- Target 2: Fleet operators (immediate ROI from demand charge reduction)

- Target 3: Municipal parking (government incentives + public relations)

- Target 4: Highway rest stops (high-traffic, premium pricing opportunity)

- Renvolt Advantage: We own the software stack + battery optimization + government relationship expertise.

Renvolt Pricing Strategy: $0.50/kWh
-------------------------------

**Renvolt will price DC fast charging at $0.50 per kWh** at the Bear Hills pilot site. This positions Renvolt in the upper-middle range of the Canadian market, justified by the following factors:

• Lower than Tesla Supercharger ($0.65–$0.71/kWh) — attracting non-Tesla EV owners who face Tesla’s premium pricing or the $0.92/kWh non-Tesla surcharge

• Competitive with Electrify Canada ($0.40–$0.50/kWh) — but Electrify Canada has no stations near Bear Hills, eliminating direct price comparison for drivers

• Location monopoly pricing power — as the only DC fast charger for ~100 km on the QE2 corridor, Renvolt does not need to compete on price. Drivers requiring a charge have no alternative.

• Still significantly cheaper than gasoline — at $0.50/kWh, charging 40 kWh costs $20.00 for approximately 250 km of range. The equivalent gasoline cost at $1.50/litre in a vehicle averaging 8L/100km is $30.00–$45.00. EV charging remains 40–55% cheaper than gasoline.

• Price flexibility — Renvolt can reduce pricing to $0.45/kWh if utilization targets are not met after the first 90 days of operation. Starting higher preserves margin and pricing credibility.

Battery System (100 kWh LFP)
----------------------------

### Why LFP Chemistry

Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP) is selected for its superior cycle life (5,000–7,000 cycles vs. 2,000–3,000 for NMC), thermal stability (no thermal runaway risk), and declining cost. LFP is the dominant chemistry in commercial stationary storage, holding 49.8% of global market revenue share in 2025.

### Degradation Profile

|  |  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Year** | **Estimated Capacity** | **Revenue Impact** | **Action Required** |
| 1–3 | 95–100 kWh | None — full performance | Routine monitoring |
| 4–6 | 85–95 kWh | Minimal — ~5% reduction in demand charge savings | None |
| 7–9 | 75–85 kWh | Moderate — grid services revenue drops 15–20% | Evaluate adding second module |
| 10–12 | 65–80 kWh | Noticeable — replacement justified | Replace or supplement |

Key degradation factors:

• Cycle life: 5,000–7,000 cycles at 80% depth of discharge to 80% capacity

• Daily cycling: 1–2 cycles/day = ~550 cycles/year = 9–13 years to 80% capacity

• Calendar aging: ~15% capacity loss after 7 years independent of cycling

• Temperature: Cold accelerates degradation; heated enclosure required in Alberta

### Alberta Winter Impact on Battery

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Temperature** | **Capacity Impact** | **Mitigation** |
| 20–25°C (summer) | 100% — optimal | None needed |
| 0°C | 90–95% | Minor; no action |
| -20°C | 70–80% | Heated enclosure required ($5,000–$10,000) |
| -30°C | 60–70% | Heated enclosure + BMS cold management protocol |

*A heated/insulated battery enclosure ($5,000–$10,000) is mandatory for Alberta deployment. This investment extends battery life by an estimated 2–3 years by preventing extreme cold cycling damage.*

Industry Overview

The EV Charging Market in Canada
--------------------------------

Canada’s electric vehicle market is at an inflection point. The federal government has mandated that 100% of new light-duty vehicle sales must be zero-emission by 2035, creating a structural, policy-driven demand curve for EV charging infrastructure that is largely independent of market sentiment or economic cycles.

The Infrastructure Gap
----------------------

Alberta has approximately one DC fast charger for every 100 EVs on the road—well below the IEA’s recommended ratio of 1:10. This gap is most acute along highway corridors between major cities, where stretches of 50–100+ km exist without any DC fast-charging option. The QE2 Highway (Highway 2) between Edmonton and Red Deer is the most significant example: approximately 100 km without a single DC fast charger on Alberta’s busiest highway.

The Demand Charge Problem
-------------------------

Unlike residential electricity, commercial EV charging sites face demand charges—utility fees based on peak power draw, not energy consumed. For a 4-charger DC fast site, demand charges can represent 30–50% of total electricity costs, severely eroding margins. Battery co-location solves this by absorbing peak loads and presenting a lower, flatter demand profile to the utility, reducing demand charges by 50–70%. This is the core technical insight behind Renvolt’s integrated model.

Battery Sizing & Optimization Strategy
======================================

The co-located battery system is a critical differentiator for Renvolt’s EV charging sites. This section outlines the rationale for the 100 kWh specification and the demand charge economics that drive the sizing decision.

What the Battery Does
---------------------

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Function** | **How It Works** |
| Demand Charge Shaving | Utilities charge based on the highest 15-minute peak demand in a month. The battery absorbs peaks so the grid sees a lower, flatter load—reducing demand charges by 50-70%. |
| Energy Arbitrage | The battery charges from the grid during off-peak hours (overnight, $0.05-0.08/kWh) and discharges during peak hours ($0.12-0.18/kWh), capturing the price spread. |
| Grid Services Revenue | The utility pays Renvolt to discharge the battery during grid stress events (frequency regulation, demand response). Additional $500-$2,000/month. |
| Charging Continuity | During momentary grid fluctuations, the battery ensures uninterrupted charging sessions, improving customer experience and equipment longevity. |

Why 100 kWh Is the Right Size
-----------------------------

Battery sizing follows a rule of approximately 25 kWh per charger for optimal demand charge shaving. For a 4-charger pilot site, this yields 100 kWh. Three options were evaluated:

### Size Comparison Analysis

|  |  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Metric** | **50 kWh** | **100 kWh** | **200 kWh** |
| Battery Cost | $20,000-$30,000 | $40,000-$60,000 | $80,000-$120,000 |
| Peak Shaving Capability | Minimal (50 kW for 1 hr) | Strong (100 kW for 1 hr) | Maximum (200 kW for 1 hr) |
| Monthly Demand Charge | ~$3,750 | ~$2,250 | ~$2,250 |
| Annual Demand Savings | ~$9,000 | ~$27,000 | ~$33,000 |
| Grid Services Revenue | $300-$800/mo | $500-$2,000/mo | $1,000-$3,000/mo |
| Incremental ROI vs. Smaller | — | $18,000/yr for $20-30K more | $6,000/yr for $40-60K more |
| Payback on Battery Itself | 2-3 years | 1.5-2 years | 3-5 years |
| Verdict | Too small | Optimal | Diminishing returns |

Demand Charge Economics
-----------------------

Demand charges are the hidden cost killer for EV charging sites, often representing 30-50% of total electricity costs. Alberta utilities assess demand charges based on the single highest 15-minute peak in a billing period.

### Without Battery vs. With 100 kWh Battery

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Scenario** | **Without Battery** | **With 100 kWh Battery** |
| 3 chargers active simultaneously | 300 kW peak | ~150 kW net peak |
| Demand charge rate | $15/kW/month | $15/kW/month |
| Monthly demand charge | $4,500 | $2,250 |
| Annual demand charge | $54,000 | $27,000 |
| Annual savings | — | $27,000 |

*The 100 kWh battery pays for itself through demand charge savings alone in approximately 1.5-2 years, while simultaneously generating grid services revenue and providing operational resilience.*

Scalability Path
----------------

The battery system is modular by design. As the site scales, additional capacity can be added without replacing existing equipment:

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Expansion Trigger** | **Action** | **Battery Size** |
| Pilot launch (4 chargers) | Initial deployment | 100 kWh |
| Expand to 8-12 chargers | Add second 100 kWh module | 200 kWh |
| Add BaaS for casino building | Add 100-200 kWh dedicated to facility | 300-400 kWh |
| Grid services revenue exceeds $3,000/mo | Larger battery justified by utility payments | Evaluate 500+ kWh |

Starting with 100 kWh allows Renvolt to prove the unit economics at minimal capital risk, then scale storage capacity in lockstep with charger count and revenue growth. This modular approach avoids overinvestment while preserving the option to expand.
