SECTION 2: PILLAR 1 - EV CHARGING + BATTERY CO-LOCATION STRATEGY

Market Opportunity:

Alberta's EV market is at an inflection point. Government funding is flowing, but infrastructure deployment lags demand. Charging operators face a hidden economics problem: they don't understand how to optimize energy costs.

Market Reality Check:

- Government funding: $1.7M in provincial grants (2025) + federal ZEVIP program allocating $5B+ nationally

- New stations announced: 325 EV charging stations planned for Alberta in 2025-2026

- Current undersaturation: Only ~1,000 EVs in Alberta as of 2025, but 15-20% annual growth projected

- Market timing: Early players can secure premium real estate before competitors arrive

The Hidden Economics: Demand Charges

The real money in EV charging isn't in charging fees—it's in energy cost optimization. Here's why this matters:

Problem for charging operators:

- A typical DC fast charger draws 200kW when operational

- Utility companies charge "demand charges" ($15-30/kW/month) based on the single highest 15-minute peak

- If a charger hits peak usage during grid peak hours, demand charges can be $3,000-6,000/month

- On a 10-station site, demand charges become $30,000-60,000/month = $360,000-720,000/year

- For a typical charging site with $500K/year revenue, demand charges consume 30-50% of gross profit

Our Solution: Battery Co-location + Smart Scheduling

- Deploy a 100-200kWh battery at each charging site

- During low-rate hours (2am-6am), pre-charge the battery from the grid

- During peak hours, discharge battery to power chargers, avoiding demand charge peaks

- Result: Reduce demand charges by 60-80%, converting costs into profit

Revenue Streams:

1. Charging Fees: $0.30-0.50/kWh (direct to Renvolt)

2. Demand Charge Optimization: $2,000-4,000/month per site (margin improvement)

3. Grid Services Revenue: $500-1,000/month per battery (selling frequency regulation to the grid)

4. Government Grants: Subsidize 40-50% of upfront capital costs

Pillar 1 Unit Economics:

- Capital cost per site: $200K (chargers + battery + controls)

- Government grant (net cost): $100K

- Monthly revenue: $3,000-5,000

- Monthly costs: $800-1,200

- Net monthly profit: $2,000-3,800

- Payback period: 30-50 months (investor-friendly)

- IRR: 25-35% annually

Go-to-Market Strategy:

- Target 1: Gas station/convenience store chains (want to add EV charging without capex)

- Target 2: Fleet operators (immediate ROI from demand charge reduction)

- Target 3: Municipal parking (government incentives + public relations)

- Target 4: Highway rest stops (high-traffic, premium pricing opportunity)

Competitive Positioning:

- Petro-Canada/Shell: Own the real estate but lack software expertise.

- Renvolt Advantage: We own the software stack + battery optimization + government relationship expertise.

SECTION 3: PILLAR 2 - BATTERY-AS-A-SERVICE (BaaS) FOR MICROGRIDS

Market Opportunity:

Industrial sites, commercial buildings, and microgrids across Western Canada need energy storage to:

1. Reduce demand charges (like EV charging, but at larger scale)

2. Enable renewable integration (solar + wind require storage for grid stability)

3. Provide backup power during grid outages

4. Meet ESG commitments and corporate net-zero goals

Market Size:

- Total addressable market (TAM): 50,000+ industrial/commercial sites in Western Canada needing energy storage

- Current penetration: <5% (2,000 sites)

- Market growth: 20-30% annually as battery costs drop and ESG mandates intensify

- Average contract value: $250K-500K per site over 10-year contract

- Total market value: $12.5B-25B over next 10 years

Why BaaS?

Capital-intensive solutions (buying batteries outright) prevent adoption. BaaS changes the equation:

Traditional Model (Customer buys battery):

- Upfront capex: $100K-300K

- Customer bears technology risk (battery may become obsolete)

- Customer bears operational risk (maintenance, monitoring, support)

- Adoption barrier: HIGH (requires 5+ year commitment with uncertain ROI)

Renvolt BaaS Model:

- No upfront capex for customer (we own and maintain the battery)

- Monthly fee: $2,000-5,000/month (fully managed service)

- Contract term: 10-15 years (recurring revenue for Renvolt)

- Customer benefit: Predictable costs, risk transfer, guaranteed ROI

- Adoption barrier: LOW (easy to adopt, immediate savings)

BaaS Revenue Streams:

1. Service Fee: $2,000-5,000/month per customer (covers hardware depreciation + OpEx)

2. Energy Margin: Profit from optimizing site energy (buying cheap, selling expensive)

3. Ancillary Services: Demand response programs, frequency regulation (selling to grid)

4. Data Monetization: Anonymous energy data sold to utilities for grid planning

BaaS Unit Economics:

- Hardware cost: $150K per system (100-300kWh battery + controls + installation)

- Customer acquisition cost (CAC): $20K (sales, legal, deployment)

- Total upfront cost: $170K

- Monthly recurring revenue (MRR): $3,000

- Gross margin: 65% ($1,950/month)

- Payback period: 18-24 months (excellent for venture-scale business)

- 10-year contract lifetime value (LTV): $468K

- LTV:CAC ratio: 23x (venture-scale benchmark: >3x)

Go-to-Market Strategy:

1. Target industrial sites with high demand charges (paper mills, semiconductor fabrication, data centers)

2. Target solar farm operators (need storage for grid interconnection)

3. Target municipalities (ESG commitments, local economic development)

4. Partner with energy service companies (ESCOs) for white-label offerings

Competitive Landscape:

- Nuvve: Focused on vehicle-to-grid, not stationary storage. Valuation: $1.2B (24x revenue multiple)

- ABB: Large conglomerate, slow to move, expensive pricing

- Tesla Energy Services: Focused on large-scale utility projects, not commercial/industrial

- Renvolt Advantage: We are nimble, focused on SMB/commercial segment, and deeply integrated with Alberta market dynamics

Proof of Concept:

SECTION 9: RISK ANALYSIS & MITIGATION

Risk 1: Government Policy Changes

- Risk: Change in EV charging subsidies or grid regulation could reduce incentives

- Probability: Medium (government policy can shift with election cycles)

- Impact: High (could reduce Pillar 1 viability)

- Mitigation: Diversify across three pillars (Pillar 2 & 3 less dependent on subsidies); maintain government relationships; educate stakeholders

Risk 2: Battery Technology Obsolescence

- Risk: New battery technology (solid-state, sodium-ion) could disrupt second-life battery demand

- Probability: Low (technology shift is 5+ year horizon)

- Impact: Medium (second-life margins compress, but market expands)

- Mitigation: Invest in technology research; maintain partnerships with OEMs; focus on deployment (not just sourcing)

Risk 3: Competition From Well-Funded Startups

- Risk: Tesla, Aggreko, or other competitors enter Alberta market aggressively

- Probability: High (attractive market draws competitors)

- Impact: Medium (reduces pricing power, increases customer acquisition costs)

- Mitigation: Move fast to secure premium locations and customer relationships; build network effects through integrated platform

Risk 4: Supply Chain Disruption

- Risk: Charger or battery component shortages delay deployments

- Probability: Medium (supply chains remain fragile post-2020)

- Impact: Medium (delays revenue recognition, increases costs)

- Mitigation: Diversify suppliers; maintain safety stock; negotiate long-term supply agreements

Risk 5: Customer Acquisition Slower Than Projected

- Risk: Sales cycle longer than expected, adoption slower

- Probability: Medium (energy infrastructure sales are complex and lengthy)

- Impact: High (misses revenue targets, increases burn rate)

- Mitigation: Focus on pilot programs to prove ROI; align with energy service companies (ESCOs) for distribution; secure government endorsement
