# Renvolt
## Comprehensive Grid Interconnection & Energy Regulations
*Prepared: March 2026*

## 1. Executive Summary

This document details the regulatory framework governing the connection of Renvolt's Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) to the Alberta grid. Our business model—which utilizes Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) to buffer the immense grid impact of DC Fast Chargers (DCFC)—provides a unique structural advantage in bypassing the most severe regulatory and cost bottlenecks imposed by Alberta utilities.

Navigating the Alberta Utilities Commission (AUC), the Alberta Electric System Operator (AESO), and local Wires Owners (e.g., FortisAlberta, ENMAX) efficiently is paramount to our deployment velocity.

---

## 2. Provincial Regulatory Bodies & Frameworks

Any Distributed Energy Resource connecting to the Alberta Interconnected Electric System (AIES) is strictly governed by provincial bodies dedicated to maintaining grid stability.

### 2.1 AESO & The DER Roadmap
The Alberta Electric System Operator (AESO) defines DERs broadly to include anything connected to the distribution system that can supply energy, explicitly including battery storage and bidirectional EV charging. 
*   **The Push for Storage:** Under the AESO DER Roadmap, the province is actively anticipating and attempting to integrate massive influxes of battery storage to smooth out the intermittency of the booming wind/solar generation sectors in Southern Alberta.

### 2.2 AUC Rule 007: Facility Applications
The Alberta Utilities Commission (AUC) governs the legal *construction* and *operation* of energy facilities through **Rule 007**.
*   **Recent Storage Integration:** Following extensive consultations, a revised version of Rule 007 became effective in late 2025. It explicitly streamlines the information requirements for standalone "battery storage facilities."
*   **Compliance Pathway:** Renvolt must file applications outlining technical specifications, noise studies, and environmental impacts for our BESS units. 
*   **The Exemption Strategy:** Recent amendments allow for expedited approvals or outright exemptions for "small power plants and energy storage facilities" under specific kW capacity thresholds. **Strategic Priority:** Renvolt site designs (Pillar 1) should be strategically sized to fit directly beneath these exemption thresholds whenever possible to bypass multi-month AUC public hearing delays.

## 3. Wires Owner Interconnection

While the AUC approves the legal existence of the facility, the local distribution company (the "Wires Owner"—such as FortisAlberta, EPCOR, or ENMAX) approves the physical *interconnection*. This represents the most significant hurdle for traditional DCFC operators (like Electrify Canada or Petro-Canada).

### 3.1 The Interconnection Process
Connecting a heavy commercial load to the grid requires executing a formal DER Interconnection Application under the Alberta Distributed Generation Interconnection Guide.
1.  **Application Submission:** Initiating the request with load profiles.
2.  **High-Level Study:** A preliminary assessment by the utility to see if the local circuit has existing capacity.
3.  **Detailed Level Study & Upgrades:** If the high-level study fails (which happens nearly 100% of the time for an un-buffered 1-megawatt DC Fast Charging plaza), the utility explicitly models the grid impact. They will frequently rule that the local substation must be upgraded, passing costs of $500,000 to $2,000,000 directly to the applicant before connection is permitted.

### 3.2 Renvolt's Deep Structural Advantage
Because traditional competitors simply plug 350kW chargers directly into the grid, they act as massive, unpredictable load spikes, forcing Wires Owners to mandate extraordinarily expensive substation upgrades.

*   **The Battery Buffer:** Renvolt utilizes commercial battery storage to buffer this load. The site pulls a smooth, continuous, low-amperage trickle from the grid (e.g., 50kW constantly over 24 hours), storing it in the BESS. When a car plugs in, the 350kW surge is delivered from the *battery*, not the grid.
*   **Bypassing the Bottleneck:** Consequently, Renvolt's stated "Peak Metered Demand (kVA)" on our interconnection application to FortisAlberta is drastically lower than our competitors. This allows Renvolt to deploy 4-8 station DCFC plazas on existing, un-upgraded rural grid infrastructure—saving millions in capital expenditures and shaving 12-18 months off deployment timelines.

## 4. Strategic Actions for Grid Integration

To fully leverage our grid-buffering advantage, operations must:

1.  **Master the SLD Narrative:** Ensure our Single-Line Diagrams (SLDs) submitted to Fortis/ENMAX explicitly emphasize the hardware-limited input draw of our sites. The utility must mathematically agree that our peak grid draw is capped by the battery inverter's input threshold, regardless of how many cars are actively charging.
2.  **Target "Grid-Constrained" Areas:** Use AUC and AESO capacity maps to deliberately target geographic areas where the grid is completely maxed out. Competitors literally cannot build there without triggering multi-million dollar substation upgrades. Renvolt can seamlessly drop in our low-draw, battery-buffered model, capturing local monopolies in these regions.
